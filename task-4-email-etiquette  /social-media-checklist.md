# Social Media Do's and Don'ts for College Students

**Made by:** Anshul | 25BCE10665

## What to Do

1. **Make Your Presence "Hiring-Ready."** Think of your LinkedIn and GitHub as a 24/7 highlight reel. A recruiter should see a professional who is ready for the job, not a mystery they have to solve, even if they find your profile at 3:00 AM.
2. **Start with Value.** Don't just scroll through your feed; share useful information, celebrate other people's wins, or offer your own insights. Having a "pretty" profile doesn't do much for your reputation. Being a "high-value" contributor does.
3. Do "digital audits" on a regular basis. Privacy settings aren't something you can just set and forget. Check your public profiles from an Incognito tab every few months to see what a stranger (or a future boss) sees.
4. Use the "Screenshot Rule." Before you hit "post," think that someone, somewhere, will take a screenshot of it. Keep that screenshot in your drafts if you wouldn't want it pinned to a bulletin board in your college hallway or office lobby.
5. **Be a "Source Snob."** In a time of deepfakes and clickbait, what you share affects how trustworthy you are. Before you share news, make sure it's true by checking official sources. It doesn't matter if you're the first to post "news" if it's not real.
---

## What Not to Do

1. "Don't Post in the Heat." The internet is not a good place to let off steam. Anger doesn't last long, but a digital rant about a coworker or professor can stay in your Google search results for years. Don't take a keyboard; go for a walk.
2. **Don't Share Your "Live" Life.** Don't tell people where you are, what you're doing, or any private IDs. It's always better to post about that cool event *after* you've left for your own safety and privacy.
3. **Don't Fall for "Skill Inflation."** Listing every programming language you've ever looked at is tempting, but having "technical debt" on your resume is bad. Don't say you have a skill on your profile if you can't explain it in person.
4. Don't be a "Panic-Forwarder." Don't share exam leaks, campus rumors, or "urgent" warnings that aren't linked to an official source. It's much more professional to be a source of calm than to be a source of noise.
5.  **Don’t Blur the Line Between "Group Chat" and "Public Forum."** Sarcasm, slang, and aggressive debates often lose their nuance in text. Maintain a tone on professional platforms that reflects how you’d speak in a formal board meeting, not a Discord server.

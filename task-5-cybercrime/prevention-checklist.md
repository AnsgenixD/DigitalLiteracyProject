# Best Ways to Keep Your Digital Life Clean and Your Things Safe

1. Basic digital defense strategies* **Preventing Typo-Squatting and Spoofing:** Domain masking or small typos (like *linked1n.com* instead of *linkedin.com*) are common ways that threat actors trick people. Before you enter any information, always check the exact Uniform Resource Locator (URL) and make sure that Transport Layer Security (HTTPS) is turned on.
* **Managing Credentials with Zero Trust:** Legitimate organizations don't trust your passwords or One-Time Passwords (OTPs) at all. Your authenticators will never be asked for by a bank, university, or IT department. Take any such request as a sign that your security has been broken.
* Zero-Trust Credential Management: Real banks, universities, and IT departments won’t ever ask for your password or OTP. If someone does, that’s a big red flag—just walk away. Always treat any such requests as a sign something’s not right.

Implementing Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Passwords just don’t cut it anymore. MFA—using authentication apps or SMS codes—adds an extra layer of safety. Turn it on for things like your email, GitHub, or banking apps. This way, even if someone gets your password, they can’t get in without that second check.

Passphrase Complexity: Skip the birthday passwords or pet names. Instead, use long, unpredictable passphrases created by a trustworthy password manager. Longer passes mean brute-force attacks hit a wall.

Securing FinTech and P2P Transactions

Understanding Asymmetric Payment Authorization: Here’s the thing—UPI fraudsters often trick people using the “collect” or “pull” feature. Just remember: your UPI PIN is only for sending money, not for receiving it. If someone wants you to enter your PIN to get paid, it’s a scam.

Protecting Financial Identifiers: Keep things like CVV numbers, card expiry dates, and UPI PINs to yourself. Legitimate bank staff or customer service reps never need this info over the phone or by email. If anyone asks, don’t share—period.

Incident Response and Mitigation Protocols

If you think you’ve been hacked or scammed, every second counts.

The "Golden Hour" of Reporting: The faster you act, the better your chances are. Fast reporting can help the authorities freeze fraudulently transferred money before it disappears.

Centralized Reporting: File your complaint right away at the National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal (cybercrime.gov.in). Don’t wait.

Emergency Contact: For urgent cases—like if money just left your account—call the National Cybercrime Helpline at 1930. It’s open 24/7. They can help trace and block fake transfers fast.

4. References & Advisory Sources

* Computer Emergency Response Team India (CERT-In): Check their national advisories for tips on using multi-factor authentication and keeping your personal devices secure.

* Reserve Bank of India (RBI): The “RBI Kehta Hai” public awareness campaign covers how to stay safe during digital transactions and explains important UPI safety protocols.

* Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA), Government of India: Find their guidelines for quick incident response and learn how to use the 1930 financial fraud helpline.

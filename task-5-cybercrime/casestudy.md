# Case Study: Phishing and Social Engineering to Get Credentials
**Attack Vector:** Phishing/Social Engineering

**Written by:** Anshul Dhavale | 25BCE10665

### 1. An Overview of the Idea
Phishing is a type of social engineering in which bad people pretend to be real organizations to trick people into giving up private information. Instead of hacking with brute force, attackers use human psychology, like trust, fear, and urgency, to get around security measures and steal credentials, financial information, or personal identities.
   **The Link That Is a Weapon** There is a link in the email that hides a bad URL. It sends the victim to a fake webpage that looks exactly like the login screen for the university's official student portal.
   **Data Capture:** The student quickly types in their username and password because they are afraid of losing access to their coursework. 
   **Abuse:** The fake site records the student's keystrokes and then sends them to the real university homepage to avoid raising any red flags. At the same time, the attacker logs into the real account right away.




### 3. Vulnerability Profile (Main Targets)

Attackers often go after university students and staff because:  
  **High-Stress Environments:** When students are under a lot of academic pressure, they are more likely to skim through emails and miss obvious red flags.

  **Authority Compliance:** People are taught by society to follow orders from "IT Services" or "Administration."

  **Device Overlap:** Students often check their email on their phones, where shortened URLs and hidden sender addresses make it harder to spot fake domains.

### 4. The Effects and Fallout of the Threat


If one account is hacked, it can have a chain reaction:

* **Moving Sideways:** Attackers use the hacked student email to send professors, administrators, or other students very convincing phishing links that get past internal spam filters.

* **Data Extortion:** Getting into student portals can give you access to private information, schedules, and possibly even financial aid information.

* **Damage to the Institution:** If the attacker uses the account to get into the university's larger network, it could lead to the installation of ransomware or the theft of intellectual property.


* **Disruption in Personal Life:** The victim has to deal with the immediate stress of locking down accounts, the risk of identity theft, and losing access to important academic platforms.
